Write the story opening here. Lead with the most compelling detail or moment. Keep the first paragraph short and punchy to draw readers in. Aim for 2-3 sentences maximum.

Follow with a second paragraph that provides context. Who is involved? Where did this happen? Why does it matter? This sets up the rest of the story without giving everything away.

A Subhead Breaks Up the Story

Use subheads to organize longer stories into clear sections. Each section should cover one idea or scene. Readers scan before they read, and subheads give them permission to keep going.

Keep paragraphs short. Three to four sentences is ideal for web reading. White space is your friend. A wall of text will lose readers before they reach the heart of the story.

“Pull quotes highlight the most powerful moment in the story. Use a direct quote from a participant, teacher, or community member.”

— [Speaker Name], [Role or Title]

Continue the story after the pull quote. This section often covers the outcome, the impact, or the next chapter. Connect the personal story back to the foundation’s mission.

Why This Matters

End with the “so what.” Help the reader understand what this story means for the broader community. How does this work connect to what the foundation is building? What can the reader do next?

A strong closing paragraph ties the personal story to the collective mission. It should leave the reader feeling moved to act, share, or learn more.

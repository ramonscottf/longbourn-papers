The Art of Letterpress

A 550-year tradition of pressing ink into paper—one impression at a time.

A Living History

Born in Gutenberg’s Workshop

Around 1440, in the German city of Mainz, a goldsmith named Johannes Gutenberg changed the world forever. His invention of movable type and the mechanical printing press ignited a revolution—one that would spread knowledge, democratize literacy, and reshape civilization itself.

For the first time, individual letters cast in metal could be arranged, inked, and pressed into paper with extraordinary precision. Each impression carried the physical weight of its creation: the bite of metal into fiber, the bloom of ink under pressure. The Gutenberg Bible, completed around 1455, remains one of the most beautiful printed works ever produced—proof that this was never merely a mechanical process, but an art form from its very first day.

Over the next five and a half centuries, letterpress evolved through the hands of countless master printers, from Aldus Manutius in Renaissance Venice to William Morris and his Kelmscott Press in Victorian England. Each generation refined the craft while preserving its essential truth: that the meeting of ink, type, and paper under pressure creates something no other method can replicate.

At Longbourn Papers, we are the inheritors of this tradition. Our studio carries forward Gutenberg’s legacy—not as museum reproduction, but as living craft, creating stationery that honors 550 years of printing history with every single impression.

The Process

From First Sketch to Final Ribbon

Every piece of Longbourn stationery passes through six meticulous stages, each demanding patience, precision, and an unwavering respect for the craft.

Design

Every motif begins as a hand-drawn illustration—botanical studies, typographic compositions, and decorative flourishes sketched in pencil, then refined in ink. Each design is created specifically for the letterpress medium, with line weights and spacing calibrated to produce the deepest, most beautiful impression possible. What looks right on a screen rarely translates directly to the press; the design must anticipate the physics of impression.

Plate Making

The finished design is transferred onto a photopolymer plate—a light-sensitive material that, when exposed and washed, creates a raised surface identical to traditional metal type. The plate is carefully inspected under magnification, then trimmed and mounted to a base that locks into the press bed with absolute precision. A fraction of a millimeter matters.

Ink Mixing

We mix our inks by hand, blending soy-based pigments on a glass slab until the hue is exactly right. Soy-based inks produce richer, more saturated color than petroleum alternatives, and they are gentler on the environment. Each color is tested on cotton paper stock before a single production sheet is printed—because ink behaves differently on cotton than on wood-pulp paper, and we demand perfection.

Impression

This is the heart of letterpress: the moment when plate meets paper under thousands of pounds of pressure. Each sheet of cotton paper is fed into the press individually, by hand. The rollers distribute ink across the plate, the platen closes, and the design is pressed—not printed on top of, but physically pressed into—the paper. The result is a tactile debossing you can feel with your fingertips, a gentle sculptural quality unique to letterpress.

Die Cutting

After printing, each piece is die-cut to its final shape using custom steel-rule dies. Gift tags receive their signature rounded corners and ribbon holes; cards are trimmed to precise dimensions with clean, deckle-free edges. The die must align perfectly with the printed impression—another step where handcraft and attention to detail determine the quality of the finished piece.

Finishing

Every card is paired with a coordinating envelope. Gift tags and grand tags are threaded with hand-tied satin ribbon—each bow tied individually, each tail trimmed at a matching angle. The finished pieces are inspected one final time, then nestled into packaging designed to protect and present them beautifully. From studio to your hands, nothing is automated.

The Impression

“There is a profound beauty in the physical impression—the moment when ink is not merely placed upon paper, but pressed into it, becoming part of its very fiber.”

Letterpress is the only printing method that sculpts the paper itself. Each impression is a small act of permanence—a mark that cannot be erased, a gesture that endures.

Our Materials

Cotton, Ink & Satin

The materials we choose are as intentional as the craft itself. Every element is selected not only for its beauty, but for how it performs under the pressure of the press.

100% Cotton Paper. Our paper is made entirely from cotton fiber—no trees are harvested, no wood pulp is used. Cotton paper is naturally soft, remarkably strong, and archival in quality, meaning it will not yellow or deteriorate over decades. Most importantly, cotton fiber accepts the letterpress impression with extraordinary grace, creating that deep, sculptural debossing that defines our stationery. The weight is substantial: you feel it the instant you pick up a Longbourn card.

Soy-Based Inks. We use soy-based inks formulated with rich, lightfast pigments. These inks produce deeper saturation and truer color than conventional petroleum-based alternatives. They sit beautifully in the impression left by the press, pooling slightly at the edges of each letter and illustration to create the subtle ink variation that is a hallmark of genuine letterpress printing.

Satin Ribbon. Our gift tags and grand tags are finished with double-faced satin ribbon, hand-tied in our studio. The ribbon is chosen to complement each design’s color palette—a final tactile detail that elevates the unboxing experience and signals that this is something made with care.

The Difference

Why Letterpress?

In a world of inkjet printers and laser toner, letterpress stands apart—not as nostalgia, but as a fundamentally different way of putting ink on paper.

The Tactile Impression

Digital printing sits on the surface of paper. Letterpress presses into it. Run your fingertip across a Longbourn card and you will feel the design—each letter gently debossed, each illustration sculpted into the cotton fiber. This tactile dimension transforms a piece of stationery from something you see into something you experience. It is the difference between reading a message and holding one.

The Beautiful Imperfection

No two letterpress impressions are perfectly identical. The ink distributes with slight, natural variation—pooling a fraction deeper in one letter, kissing a touch lighter at the edge of a petal. These are not flaws. They are the signatures of a handmade process, proof that a human hand fed each sheet and a physical press made each mark. In a world of pixel-perfect uniformity, this honest variation is what makes letterpress feel alive.

The Weight of Real Paper

Mass-produced cards are printed on thin, wood-pulp stock that bends and creases. Our 100% cotton paper has a heft and presence that announces itself before a single word is read. When someone receives a Longbourn card, they notice the weight first—then the texture, then the impression, then the design. Every sense is engaged before they even begin to read your words. That is the power of letterpress on cotton: it gives your message a physical form worthy of its meaning.

“Letterpress is slow on purpose. It asks you to be deliberate—about the words you choose, the paper you hold, and the person you send it to.”

Alexandra, Founder

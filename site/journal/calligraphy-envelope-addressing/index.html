Addressing Envelopes in Calligraphy: Pencil to Postbox

Ellie Hartwell · May 20, 2026 · 3 min read

The envelope is calligraphy's recital. Practice sheets are private; the envelope goes out into the world, gets handled by strangers, and lands in front of exactly the person you wanted to impress — carrying your hand, your spacing decisions, and any smudge you committed at line three. It is also, wonderfully, the most forgiving format in the craft: four or five short lines, generous margins, and a recipient predisposed to delight. Here is the complete method, from pencil to postbox — including the unglamorous parts about the postal service.

Guidelines: the non-negotiable

Nobody — nobody — freehands straight, evenly-spaced envelope lines. The pros pencil or template every envelope, and so will you. Three honest methods:

  • Pencil + ruler (works on everything): draw your baselines lightly with a hard pencil — 2H or the beloved Palomino Blackwing alternative, a mechanical 0.5 — wait a full 24 hours after inking, then erase with a clean white eraser. Erase sooner and you'll drag half-cured ink into a ghost smear; the patience is the technique.
  • Light box + guide sheet (light envelopes): print one master guide card, slip it inside or beneath the envelope on a cheap LED light pad, and the lines shine through. Fastest method for a stack of fifty.
  • The laser-guide trick (a stationer's favorite): print faint guidelines directly onto a template card sized to your envelope; for single-layer envelopes, the card inside + daylight window works as a poor man's light box.

Centering without tears

Centered addresses look effortless and are entirely premeditated. The working method: write the address out on scrap first, in the same script and size. For each line, find its middle character, mark the envelope's vertical center with a pencil tick, and start that middle character on the tick — writing outward in your mind from the center. After a dozen envelopes you'll eyeball it; until then, the scrap-draft is the difference between centered and almost. (The left-aligned address block, staggered stepwise line by line, is the classic alternative — equally correct, far more forgiving, and how most wedding calligraphers price their sanity.)

Composing the lines

Names get the glory — largest script, any flourish you can honestly land. The street, city, and postcode lines step down in size and restraint. A pleasing default: name at full x-height with flourished capitals, address lines at two-thirds scale with minimal ornament, generous line spacing (crowded lines read as clutter from arm's length, which is where envelopes are actually viewed). Resist flourishing descenders into the line below; one collision undoes the whole performance.

The postal reality check

Your envelope must charm a human and survive a sorting machine, so a few worldly rules: keep the numerals plain — house numbers and postcodes in clear, unflourished figures (a 7 that reads as a 1 sends your masterpiece to the wrong street); keep the bottom ~16mm clear where sorting barcodes get sprayed; put the stamp corner-square and the return address small on the flap or top-left. Dark or heavily decorated envelopes may earn a hand-cancel — ask at the counter; most clerks oblige beautiful mail with visible pleasure.

Dark envelopes, white ink

Navy and hunter envelopes with white script are the format's evening wear. The inks that actually work: Dr. Ph. Martin's Bleedproof White (thick — dilute with water drop by drop to single-cream consistency) for dip pens, or the humble white gel pen (Gelly Roll 08/10) for faux calligraphy that photographs identically. Pencil guidelines become soapstone or a white charcoal pencil. Test on the envelope's flap first — dark papers vary wildly in how they take ink.

Practice run: the grocery list

Address something every day that doesn't matter — the electric bill, a note to your own future self, the recipe card. Envelopes only feel high-stakes because we save the skill for weddings; spend it on Tuesdays and the wedding stack becomes routine. (Complete beginner? Start at the beginner's guide; the drills are the strokes these addresses are made of.)

And what goes inside should keep the envelope's promise — letterpress cards that answer the calligraphy, plus the What Do I Write? tool when the beautiful envelope is ready before the words are. Addressing for a wedding? Pair this with the wedding card guide.

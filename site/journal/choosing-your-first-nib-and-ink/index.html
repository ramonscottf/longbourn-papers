Choosing Your First Nib and Ink (The Under-$30 Kit)

Scott Foster · April 15, 2025 · 3 min read

Walk into pointed-pen calligraphy unadvised and the shop will happily sell you forty dollars of wrong things: a nib too flexible for a beginner's hand, ink that feathers on the paper you own, a holder shaped for a script you're not learning. The correct first kit costs under thirty dollars, fits in a pencil case, and hasn't changed in decades. Here it is, item by item, with the reasoning — because knowing why is what stops you buying the wrong upgrades later.

The nib: Nikko G, and it isn't close

The Nikko G — a Japanese steel nib originally made for manga artists — is the universal right answer to "first nib," recommended by essentially every working teacher. The reasons are mechanical: it's stiff enough that your untrained pressure won't splay it into railroad tracks, flexible enough to produce real thick-thin contrast, and its tines are hardy enough to survive the upstroke-stabbing every beginner inflicts on paper. Softer, swoonier nibs (the Brause Rose, the Hunt 101) produce glorious swells in trained hands and pure frustration in new ones. Buy two Nikko Gs — nibs are consumable, a few months of regular practice each — and revisit the flexible ones in your second season.

Prep the nib before first use. New nibs ship with a factory oil coating that repels ink — the cause of the classic "why won't ink stay on my nib" first-night crisis. Scrub it with toothpaste on an old toothbrush, or pass it briefly through a flame and wipe. Thirty seconds; skip it and nothing works.

The holder: straight first, oblique when slant calls

A basic straight holder (cork or plastic, a few dollars) is correct for learning strokes, drills, and upright-to-gently-slanted modern styles. The strange flanged oblique holder exists for one job: holding the nib at copperplate's steep 55° slant without contorting your wrist. If copperplate's formal discipline is your destination — here's how to decide — an inexpensive plastic oblique (Speedball's is fine) is worth adding early. Right-handers genuinely benefit; many left-handers do beautifully with a straight holder and adjusted paper rotation.

The ink: sumi, and a jam jar of water

First bottle: Japanese sumi ink (Moon Palace or Kuretake) or Higgins Eternal. Sumi flows generously, dries to a dignified matte black with body, and behaves on modest paper; Higgins is thinner, cheaper, everywhere, and perfectly honest to learn on. Avoid, for now: India inks with heavy shellac (they clog and crust on the nib), fountain-pen inks (formulated too thin for dip work), and anything glittering (later; you've earned nothing yet). Decant an inch into a small heavy jar — dipping into the retail bottle ends in tragedy exactly once. Keep a second jar of water and a rag: rinse the nib every few minutes as ink dries and crusts, and dry it — steel nibs rust with enthusiasm.

The paper: the secret most important item

Here is the trade secret that saves beginners from despair: most "I'm terrible at this" is actually bad paper. Ordinary copy paper is a sponge — hairlines feather into fuzz, fibers lift and jam the tines, ink bleeds through. The fixes are cheap: Rhodia pads (the beloved standard — impossibly smooth), HP Premium 32lb laser paper (the budget legend; a ream lasts a year), or Canson Marker paper for seeing guidelines through. Test every future paper purchase with one wet hairline before committing. When your practice graduates to real correspondence, cotton letterpress stock takes dip-pen ink like it was born for it — which, around here, it was.

The complete first kit

  • 2 × Nikko G nibs
  • 1 straight holder (+ a plastic oblique if copperplate calls)
  • Sumi ink or Higgins Eternal, decanted into a jar
  • Rhodia pad or a ream of 32lb laser paper
  • Water jar, rag, old toothbrush (nib prep + cleaning)

Under thirty dollars, all of it. Everything else the shop offers — gold ink, walnut crystals, vintage flexible nibs, the beautiful ash oblique holders — is real and wonderful and belongs in month three, purchased as rewards, when your hands can cash the cheques the tools write.

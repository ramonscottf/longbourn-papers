The Craft

Five hundred years of printing heritage, one sheet at a time.

The Heritage

A 500-Year-Old Art Form

Letterpress printing began around 1440, when Johannes Gutenberg developed movable type in Mainz, Germany. For the next five centuries, every book, newspaper, broadside, and calling card was printed this way: raised type or image, pressed into paper with force and ink.

The industrial revolution brought offset and digital printing — faster, cheaper, infinitely scalable. Letterpress should have died. Instead, something unexpected happened: as the world went digital, people began to crave what machines couldn’t replicate — the deep, tactile impression of type pressed into soft cotton paper.

Today, letterpress is a fine-art printing method prized for its texture, depth, and craftsmanship. Each piece carries the evidence of its own making — a quality that no laser printer or inkjet will ever reproduce.

The Press

The Heidelberg Windmill

Our press is a Heidelberg Windmill — a platen press manufactured in Germany in the 1960s. It is widely considered the finest platen press ever made: a mechanical masterpiece of cams, gears, and grippers that feeds, prints, and delivers each sheet with metronomic precision.

The “Windmill” name comes from the press’s distinctive feeding arms, which rotate like windmill blades, picking up a single sheet from the feed pile, swinging it into the press for impression, and then depositing it on the delivery pile. The motion is mesmerizing — and it has remained essentially unchanged for over sixty years.

We run our Heidelberg at a deliberate pace. Speed is not the point. Impression quality is. Each sheet receives the full force of the press, creating that deep, three-dimensional bite into the paper that defines true letterpress.

The Paper

Crane Lettra Cotton Paper

We print exclusively on Crane Lettra — 100% cotton paper made by Crane & Co., an American mill with over two centuries of papermaking heritage. Crane Lettra was developed specifically for letterpress printing, and it remains the gold standard.

Cotton fiber gives the paper its extraordinary softness and strength. Unlike wood-pulp papers, cotton won’t yellow or become brittle with age. It has a warm, substantial hand — you feel the quality the moment you pick it up.

The deep impression that defines our work is only possible because of this paper. Cotton fibers compress and hold their shape under the force of the press, creating a debossed surface you can see in raking light and feel with your fingertips. It is the tactile signature of letterpress — and the reason people keep our cards for years.

The Ink

Hand-Mixed, Pantone Matched

Color matters. Every ink we use is hand-mixed to match a specific Pantone shade — the global standard for color accuracy. This means the sage green on your note cards is exactly the sage green we designed, not an approximation.

Letterpress ink is oil-based and slow-drying, formulated to transfer cleanly from plate to paper under pressure. It sits on the surface of the cotton fibers rather than being absorbed, which gives letterpress its characteristic crisp, saturated appearance.

We mix small batches and test each color on the actual paper stock before a production run begins. It is painstaking, but it ensures that every card meets our standard — because once ink meets paper under 20 tons of pressure, there are no second chances.

The Process

From Design to Delivery

Every card passes through six stages, each performed by hand. There are no shortcuts.

Design

Each design begins as a sketch before moving to digital layout. Typography is set, spacing is refined, and every element is considered for how it will translate under pressure on cotton paper.

Plate Making

The digital design is output onto a photopolymer plate — a light-sensitive material that hardens where exposed to UV light, creating a raised printing surface. The plate is mounted on the press bed with precise registration.

Press Setup

Ink is mixed to the exact Pantone formula and distributed across the ink rollers. Packing is adjusted behind the platen to control impression depth. Test sheets are run until color density and registration are perfect.

Hand-Feeding

Sheets of Crane Lettra are loaded into the feed pile, and the Heidelberg begins its rhythm: feed, print, deliver. Each sheet is picked up, pressed with the full force of the platen, and set down — one at a time. For multi-color designs, the entire process is repeated for each color.

Quality Check

Every printed sheet is inspected by hand. We check for consistent ink coverage, clean impression, proper registration, and any flaws. Sheets that don’t meet our standard are pulled. There is no “close enough.”

Finishing & Packaging

Printed sheets are die-cut or hand-trimmed to their final size, scored for folding, and paired with envelopes. Each set is wrapped in tissue, sealed, and packaged with the same care that went into printing it.
